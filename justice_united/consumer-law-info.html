Civil Protection

Consumer protection is the practice of safeguarding buyers of goods and services, and the public, against unfair practices in the marketplace. Consumer protection measures are often established by law.

How do I lodge a consumer complaint in South Africa?

Complaints must be in writing and can be lodged directly on the ICASA website or a complaints form can be completed and emailed to consumer@icasa.org.za or faxed to 012 568 3444.

What is consumer protection SA?

The Consumer Protection Act provides for quick and effective complaint resolution for both Consumer and businesses. Non-compliance sanctions include fines, imprisonment for 12 months or in the case of private information disclosure, imprisonment for 10 years.

What are the five consumer rights according to the Consumer Protection Act?

Right to Fair and Honest Dealing; Right to Fair, Just and Reasonable Terms and Conditions; Right to Fair Value, Good Quality and Safety; and Right to Accountability by Suppliers.

Can a store refuse to give a refund in South Africa?

There is no general right of return.

Who does the consumer protection act not apply to?

The Act will not apply to transactions where the consumer is a juristic person with an asset value or annual turnover of more than a threshold value determined by the Minister (section 6).

More About Consumer Protection

The Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 aims:

  • to promote a fair, accessible and sustainable marketplace for consumer products and services and for that purpose to establish national norms and standards relating to consumer protection,
  • to provide for improved standards of consumer information,
  • to prohibit certain unfair marketing and business practices,
  • to promote responsible consumer behaviour,
  • to promote a consistent legislative and enforcement framework relating to consumer transactions and agreements,
  • to establish the National Consumer Commission,
  • to repeal
    1. sections 2 to 13 and sections 16 to 17 of the Merchandise Marks Act, 1941 (Act No. 17 of 1941),
    2. the Business Names Act, 1960 (Act No. 27 of 1960),
    3. the Price Control Act, 1964 (Act No. 25 of 1964),
    4. the Sales and Service Matters Act, 1964 (Act No. 25 of 1964),
    5. the Trade Practices Act. 1976 (Act No. 76 of 1976),
    6. the Consumer Affairs (Unfair Business Practices) Act, 1988 (Act No. 71 of 1988), and
  • to make consequential amendments to various other Acts; and
  • to provide for related incidental matters.
