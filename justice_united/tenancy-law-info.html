Tenancy Law

The Rental Housing Act was introduced to regulate the relationship between landlords and tenants. Its primary goal is to ensure fair and equitable treatment for both parties, as well as to promote responsible renting practices.

Legal remedies for tenants in case of landlord non-compliance

f your landlord fails to comply with the provisions of the Rental Housing Act, you have legal remedies available to protect your rights as a tenant. These remedies include:
  1. Reporting to the Rental Housing Tribunal: The Rental Housing Tribunal is a governmental body established to resolve disputes between landlords and tenants.
    If you believe your rights have been violated, you can lodge a complaint with the tribunal for investigation and resolution.
  2. Withholding rent: In certain circumstances, tenants may be entitled to withhold rent if the landlord fails to fulfil their obligations under the rental agreement.
    However, it’s crucial to seek legal advice before taking this step, as there are specific conditions that must be met.
  3. Seeking damages: If you have suffered financial or other losses due to your landlord’s non-compliance, you may be entitled to seek damages through legal action.
    Consult with a lawyer to understand the process and assess the viability of a claim.

Steps to take before signing a rental agreement

Before signing a rental agreement, it’s crucial to take certain steps to protect your rights as a tenant. These steps include:
  1. Reading and understanding the rental agreement: Carefully read through the entire rental agreement to ensure that you understand all the terms and conditions.
    If there are any clauses that you’re uncertain about, seek legal advice before signing.
  2. Inspecting the property: Before moving in, thoroughly inspect the property and document any existing damages or issues.
    Take photos or videos as evidence, and provide a copy to the landlord.
    This will help protect you from being held responsible for pre-existing damages.
  3. Clarifying any uncertainties: If there are any uncertainties or ambiguities in the rental agreement, seek clarification from the landlord before signing.
    It’s important to have a clear understanding of your obligations and the landlord’s responsibilities.

Can a landlord lock out a tenant in South Africa?

In South Africa, a landlord cannot immediately evict you without following the due processes.
If the landlord has formally cancelled your lease agreement and asked you to move out,
and you do not, they can go to court to get an eviction order. This process can take six to eight months.

Where can I report my landlord in South Africa?

1. Personally at the Rental Housing Tribunal (RHT) Office.
2. Send a complaint electronically to the RHT or established Rental Housing Information Office (RHIO).

Can a tenant refuse to pay rent?

If the tenant fails to pay their rent, a written notice of breach is the first step.
If the tenant still fails to pay their rent, you can apply to the court for an eviction order.
Do not take any steps to evict the tenant without first obtaining a court order.

Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999

The Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999 intends:
  • to define the responsibility of Government in respect of rental housing property;
  • to create mechanisms to promote the provision of rental housing property;
  • to promote access to adequate housing through creating mechanisms to ensure the proper functioning of the rental housing market;
  • to make provision for the establishment of Rental Housing Tribunals;
  • to define the functions, powers and duties of such Tribunals;
  • to lay down general principles governing conflict resolution in the rental housing sector;
  • to provide for the facilitation of sound relations between tenants and landlords and for this purpose to lay down general requirements relating to leases;
  • to repeal the Rent Control Act, 1976; and
  • to provide for matters connected therewith.

Understanding Your Rights as A Tenant

As a tenant, it’s essential to know your rights under the Act. These rights include:
  1. Right to a written rental agreement: According to the Act, landlords are required to provide tenants with a written rental agreement that clearly outlines the terms and conditions of the tenancy.
    This agreement should include details such as the rental amount, payment terms, duration of the lease, and any additional responsibilities of both parties.
  2. Right to a habitable dwelling: The Act ensures that tenants have the right to live in a habitable and safe dwelling. Landlords are responsible for maintaining the property in a good condition and ensuring that it meets the necessary health and safety standards.
  3. Right to privacy: Tenants have the right to privacy, and landlords are not allowed to enter the rented premises without proper notice or without a valid reason. The Act specifies that landlords must give tenants at least 24 hours notice before entering the property, except in cases of emergency.

Responsibilities of Landlords Under the Rental Housing Act

While tenants have specific rights, landlords also have responsibilities under the Act. These responsibilities include:
  1. Maintaining the property: Landlords are obligated to maintain the property in a good state of repair and ensure that it is habitable. This includes addressing any repairs, conducting regular maintenance, and providing essential services such as water and electricity.
  2. Adhering to rental agreements: Landlords must comply with the terms and conditions outlined in the rental agreement. This includes collecting rent on time, providing proper notice for any changes or termination of the lease, and respecting the tenant’s right to peaceful enjoyment of the property.
  3. Returning the rental deposit: At the end of the tenancy, landlords are required to return the tenant’s deposit within a reasonable time frame, minus any deductions for damages beyond normal wear and tear. The Act provides guidelines on how the deposit should be handled to protect both parties’ interests.
